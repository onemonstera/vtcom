2-Month Plant Timelapse

// the project

I filmed an indoor plant continuously for approximately two months to produce a smooth, uninterrupted timelapse video. The final result: about 8 seconds of footage.

On the surface, it sounds simple — point a camera at a plant and wait. In practice, it required more upfront planning than most software projects I've worked on.

// the constraints
  • Everything had to stay perfectly constant for the entire duration — lighting, power, airflow, camera placement
  • A single millimeter of camera movement would break the continuity and make the timelapse unwatchable
  • Once the setup was locked in, nothing could be touched
  • The camera couldn't be moved to retrieve footage — ejecting the memory card wasn't an option
// how I approached it
  • Designed a remote data retrieval method so the camera never had to be physically touched
  • Added an external power bank as a backup in case of power cuts — highly unlikely, but unacceptable risk
  • Did full test runs before committing to the two-month window to validate the setup
  • Pre-planned failure scenarios: what happens if the camera shifts, if power cuts, if something in the environment changes
// the realization

I didn't think much of it at the time — it just felt like the obvious way to approach the problem. It wasn't until later that I recognized the pattern: failure-mode analysis, redundancy planning, contingency scenarios for unlikely-but-catastrophic events.

That's the same mental model I use every day working on enterprise backup and restore systems. I'd just applied it to a plant without realizing it.

// the bigger point

The best systems hide their complexity. Nobody watching the timelapse sees the planning, the redundancy, the test runs, or the obsessive constraint management. They just see a plant growing. That's the goal — in engineering, in design, in UX. All the work should be invisible in the final result.
